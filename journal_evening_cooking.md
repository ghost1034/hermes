The garlic is sizzling in the pan, and the rich, sharp smell of ginger and soy sauce is filling up the kitchen. We’re making General Tso’s chicken tonight—my absolute favorite—and I’m on vegetable chopping duty while Dad handles the heavy cast-iron wok. Mom is setting the dining room table, picking out the good plates, and actively laughing at my incredibly clumsy knife skills. I am definitely cutting these green bell peppers way too thick and completely unevenly, but nobody really cares at all.

I desperately need this break tonight. My brain feels completely fried from the constant, overwhelming hum of everything going on in my head lately, so deliberately stepping away from the desk to just stand here and chop scallions is exactly the kind of grounding, manual task I’m craving. The kitchen is incredibly warm, slightly chaotic, and loud in the best way possible.

We are entirely focused on getting this meal together as a team. The hot wok is hissing violently as the coated chicken hits the peanut oil, and the dark sauce is bubbling away on the back burner, getting perfectly thick, sweet, and sticky. I am actively trying to soak in every single physical detail of this evening. I am moving out to Berkeley so soon, and the reality of that transition is hanging in the air. My cardboard boxes are sitting half-packed upstairs in my room, but right now, I am completely ignoring them.

Tonight is strictly about being here, right now, with my parents in our family home. We’re standing around the kitchen island, sneaking burning-hot pieces of crispy chicken before they even hit the sauce, jokingly arguing over who gets the last scoop of jasmine rice. It’s just a normal weekday night dinner, but it feels so monumental. I am so intensely grateful for this exact present moment. The food is finally ready, and we are carrying steaming bowls of rice and plates of spicy, sweet chicken to the table. We are sitting down to eat together, and I am just profoundly happy to be exactly where I am right now.
